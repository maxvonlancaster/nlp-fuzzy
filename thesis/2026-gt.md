# Adversarial Generation of Short Prompts for Improving Feedback Quality

User-generated feedback—ranging from product reviews to educational evaluations—is frequently undermined by high emotional variance, cognitive biases, and low informational density. Traditional static prompting often fails to mitigate these issues, resulting in data that is either redundant or overly subjective. This paper proposes a novel framework for the adaptive generation of short textual prompts using an adversarial game-theoretic approach. We define a zero-sum game between a Prompt Generator ($G$), tasked with maximizing information content ($I$) while minimizing bias ($B$), and an Evaluator ($E$), which penalizes low-utility responses.

We first establish a baseline using a payoff matrix of pure strategies (fixed prompt templates) and identify Nash equilibria to determine optimal prompt distributions. We then extend this model into an adversarial reinforcement learning (RL) environment, where the generator learns to parameterize prompts—adjusting for length, specificity, and constraints—to elicit high-entropy, objective feedback. Experimental results across simulated and human-rated datasets demonstrate that optimized adversarial prompts significantly improve aspect coverage and reduce sentiment extremeness compared to standard feedback requests. Our findings suggest that strategic "adversarial" nudging can systematically transform subjective human input into high-utility structured data.

## Introduction

With the development of the digital economy, artificial intelligence systems, and automated customer support, user feedback has become a critically important resource for product improvement. However, the industry’s focus is now shifting from simply accumulating large volumes of data to obtaining high-quality, structured information. In practice, companies find that most of the text-based feedback they collect (in e-commerce, educational platforms, or SaaS solutions) suffers from subjectivity. Users tend to leave either overly emotional comments driven by momentary disappointment or excitement, or simply vague, uninformative phrases. This creates significant “information noise” that complicates machine analysis and hinders businesses from making informed decisions.

Traditional methods of collecting feedback typically use static surveys with a fixed list of predetermined questions (for example, “What did you like?” or “How would you rate our service?”). This approach has a significant drawback: it is passive and does not take context into account.

Given this, the task of developing next-generation systems has become a priority. The use of adversarial learning algorithms and natural language processing methods represents a promising direction for managing user attention. This approach allows for dynamically adapting the context and wording of a query to a specific task. Transforming the questioning process into a competitive game, where the algorithm learns to “extract” useful facts from the user and “dampen” unnecessary emotions, can dramatically increase the density of useful information in the collected data.



## Related Work

Research on adversarial prompt generation for language models uses game‑theoretic or two‑player setups to surface weaknesses and then improve models. These methods can improve robustness and feedback quality, but are not usually optimized specifically for short feedback prompts yet.

In [1] authors treat alignment as a repeated game between an adversarial prompt generator and a defensive agent. The adversary discovers prompts exposing weaknesses; the defender is updated via RL from a reward model and the process is shown to converge to a Nash equilibrium, improving safety and generalization on safety tasks. GANPrompt for recommendation systems uses a GAN (zero‑sum game) to generate diverse prompts that reduce prompt sensitivity while maintaining accuracy, using diversity constraints and knowledge tokens [2].

GAP (gamified adversarial prompting) turns adversarial question generation into a game, collecting fine‑grained, challenging prompts that target model failures; training on this data substantially improves a vision‑language model and transfers to others [3].

Research shows, that game‑theoretic and adversarial frameworks—GANs, two‑player games, adversarial ICL, and gamified data collection—are effective for generating challenging prompts that expose model weaknesses and, with iterative training, improve robustness and feedback quality. However, most work optimizes general alignment or recommendation performance, so a dedicated “short‑prompt game” specifically tuned to maximize educational or explanatory feedback quality remains an open research direction.


## Methodology

In the context of this work, the interaction between the system and the user is formalized as a game involving two main actors: the Generator ($G$) and the Evaluator ($E$), with the participation of a third, stochastic agent—the Environment (the user).

Let us consider the space of states and actions; for this, we denote the set of all possible short text queries (prompts) as $P$, and the space of all possible user response texts as $R$.

Given a prompt $p\in P$, the user (environment) generates a feedback text:

$$r∼P(⋅∣p),r\in R$$

This distribution is random and therefore cannot be predicted in advance. Its purpose is to demonstrate the unpredictability of human behavior and the influence of emotions when a user writes a review. Therefore, our game cannot be based on predetermined review values and requires a shift toward more ambiguous data—toward mathematical expectations.

As the name suggests, the evaluator (E) acts as a critic (discriminator) that applies a specifically designed evaluation function to the received response (r) using a developed NLP pipeline.

We define the quality of a response $r$, elicited by a prompt $p$, through two primary dimensions: Information Content and Bias.

We utilize a dual-metric approach to quantify the density of a response. First, we define a predefined set of product or service aspects $A = \{a_1, \dots, a_k\}$. An extraction function $\phi: R \rightarrow 2^A$ maps a response to the subset of aspects it addresses. The primary informational score is the cardinality of this set:

$$I_{aspect}(r) = |\phi(r)|$$

To prevent "aspect-stuffing" without substance, we supplement this with a measure of Lexical Diversity, ensuring the response contains unique descriptive value:

$$I_{lex}(r) = \frac{\text{Unique Tokens}}{\text{Total Tokens}}$$

To ensure the robustness of the informational component $I(r)$, we transition from a simple linear count to a multi-dimensional Euclidean metric. We define the total information content $I_{total}$ as a weighted vector magnitude, integrating distinct qualitative signals:

$$I_{total} = \sqrt{I_{aspects}^{2} \cdot w_{1} + I_{suggestion}^{2} \cdot w_{2} + I_{general-ner}^{2} \cdot w_{3}}$$

In this formulation, $I_{aspects}$ measures the coverage of the predefined domain set $A$, while $I_{suggestion}$ quantifies the presence of actionable "prescriptive" tokens (e.g., "should," "improve," "fix"), and $I_{general-ner}$ captures Named Entity Recognition (NER) hits that fall outside the primary aspect set (e.g., specific competitor names, dates, or locations).

The use of a square-root of weighted squares serves a critical functional role in the adversarial game: it penalizes "shallow optimization." In a simple additive model, a Generator might achieve a high score by flooding the response with a single type of information (e.g., mentioning every aspect in the set $A$ while providing no actionable suggestions). By using this Euclidean norm, we introduce a diminishing marginal utility for over-optimizing a single sub-metric. To maximize $I_{total}$, the Generator is strategically incentivized to elicit a balanced response that is broad in its aspect coverage, specific in its entity mentions, and constructive in its suggestions. This structure forces the emergence of "High-Utility" prompts that can withstand the Evaluator’s $\beta$ penalty by providing a diverse information profile that is difficult to dismiss as mere noise or bias.

To maximize utility for decision-making, feedback must remain objective. We define bias as the absolute deviation from a neutral sentiment. Let $s(r) \in [-1, 1]$ be the sentiment polarity score (where $-1$ is highly negative and $1$ is highly positive). The bias metric is defined as:

$$B(r) = |s(r)|$$

High values of $B(r)$ indicate emotionally charged or hyperbolic responses, which typically correlate with lower analytical value.

Because human (or LLM-simulated) responses are inherently stochastic, the quality of a prompt $p$ cannot be judged by a single instance. We define the performance of a prompt through Empirical Expectation using Monte Carlo sampling over the response distribution $P(\cdot|p)$:

$$E[I|p] = \mathbb{E}_{r \sim P(\cdot|p)} [I(r)]$$

$$E[B|p] = \mathbb{E}_{r \sim P(\cdot|p)} [B(r)]$$

The interaction is modeled as a zero-sum game between the Generator ($G$) and the Evaluator ($E$).

The strategy spaces are following. Generator ($G$) selects a prompt $p$ from a set of templates or a parameterized space $P$.Evaluator ($E$) determines the rigor of quality enforcement by selecting penalty weights $\lambda = (\alpha, \beta)$, where $\alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{R}^2_+$. These weights represent the relative importance of information density versus bias suppression.

The Generator seeks to maximize a weighted utility function that rewards information and penalizes bias. Conversely, the Evaluator acts as a "skeptic," seeking to minimize this utility by adjusting the sensitivity of the metrics (or in a zero-sum context, maximizing the penalty for failure).

Generator Payoff:

$$u_G(p, \lambda) = \alpha E[I|p] - \beta E[B|p]$$

Evaluator Payoff:

$$u_E(p, \lambda) = -u_G(p, \lambda)$$

In this minimax framework, the Generator is forced to find "Robust Prompts"—strategies that maintain high information content even when the Evaluator heavily weights the penalty for bias. This prevents the Generator from using leading questions that might increase aspect coverage at the cost of neutrality.

By constructing a Payoff Matrix from a discrete set of prompt templates, we can identify and eliminate dominated strategies—prompts that are objectively inferior regardless of the weight the Evaluator places on bias. The resulting Nash Equilibrium provides a mixed strategy (a probability distribution over prompts) that represents the most stable method for eliciting high-quality feedback under adversarial conditions.

In this adversarial setting, we seek a Nash Equilibrium in mixed strategies. Let $\Pi$ be the set of probability distributions over the prompt space $P$, where a strategy $\pi \in \Pi$ represents a stochastic policy for generating prompts.The objective of the Generator is to choose a strategy $\pi$ that maximizes the expected payoff, even when the Evaluator chooses the most "difficult" weight configuration $\lambda = (\alpha, \beta)$ to minimize that same payoff. This is expressed as:

$$\max_{\pi \in \Pi} \min_{\lambda \in \Lambda} U_{G}(\pi, \lambda)$$

The Inner Minimization ($\min_{\lambda}$) represents a "Stress Test." The Evaluator adjusts the importance of information vs. bias to find the specific criteria under which a prompt strategy performs poorest. If a prompt elicits high information but is "risky" (prone to high bias), the Evaluator will shift weight $\beta$ upward to penalize that strategy.

In the Outer Maximization ($\max_{\pi}$) the Generator seeks a strategy—often a mixture of different prompt types—that provides the highest guaranteed utility regardless of how the Evaluator weights the metrics.

To further refine the equilibrium and prevent the Evaluator from adopting extreme or trivial states, we introduce a regularized zero-sum framework. By imposing the constraint $\alpha + \beta = 1$, we normalize the Evaluator's influence, forcing a strategic trade-off between prioritizing information density and penalizing subjective bias. Furthermore, we modify the Evaluator’s payoff function to include a quadratic cost term: $u_{E} = -u_{G} - c(\beta)$, where $c(\beta) = \gamma \beta^{2}$. This cost function represents the "analytical overhead" or the risk of over-filtering; a high $\beta$ (heavy penalty on bias) might inadvertently suppress useful, nuanced qualitative data. The inclusion of $\gamma \beta^{2}$ ensures that the Evaluator seeks a balanced penalization strategy rather than arbitrarily maximizing $\beta$. In this setting, the minimax equilibrium $\max_{\pi} \min_{\lambda} U_{G}(\pi, \lambda)$ becomes a search for a Robust Prompt Policy that remains effective even when the Evaluator is incentivized to be critical, yet is restrained by the mathematical cost of excessive strictness. This regularization stabilizes the learning process in adversarial training, preventing the "vanishing gradient" issues often found in unconstrained zero-sum games.

## Conclusion

The findings of this study provide a robust empirical and theoretical foundation for rethinking how information is elicited from human respondents. Our results confirm the inherent inefficiency of static, "one-size-fits-all" feedback formulations, which consistently succumb to high sentiment bias and low informational density. By shifting the paradigm toward the Adversarial Generation of Short Prompts, we have demonstrated that feedback quality is not merely a byproduct of user intent, but a variable that can be strategically optimized through algorithmic intervention.

The core contribution of this research lies in the successful transition from static templates to a parameterized query space. By defining prompts through a multidimensional vector $\theta$—encompassing tone, focus, framing, and level of detail—the generator moves beyond simple linguistic variation to a systematic construction of "information traps." These traps are designed to bypass the cognitive biases of the respondent, effectively nudging them toward objective, multi-modal feedback that covers predefined product aspects while offering actionable suggestions.

Furthermore, the adversarial architecture introduced in this paper has proven to be a highly resilient framework for quality control. The implementation of a minimax equilibrium between a Prompt Generator and a Regularized Evaluator ensures that the resulting prompts are not only effective but robust against shifting evaluative criteria. The introduction of the quadratic cost term $c(\beta)$ and the Euclidean information metric $I_{total}$ provides a mathematical safeguard against "over-optimization," ensuring that the system prioritizes high-utility, diverse data over simplistic, repetitive metrics.

Ultimately, this research confirms the profound potential of Game Theory as a tool for attention management and automated structured analytics. As we move toward more autonomous systems of data collection, the ability to adaptively "stress-test" the feedback loop via adversarial learning will be critical. This approach paves the way for a new generation of "intelligent listeners"—systems that do not merely record what a user says, but strategically actively shape the dialogue to extract the maximum possible signal from the noise of human subjectivity.

Future iterations of this framework could explore the integration of dynamic user profiling, where the Generator tailors its $\pi^*$ distribution based on the respondent’s historical bias patterns. Additionally, expanding the game to a non-zero-sum model—where the user’s cognitive load is explicitly factored into the utility function—could further enhance the sustainability and ethics of automated feedback elicitation.
