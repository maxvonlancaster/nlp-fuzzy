# Development of Automatic Extraction of Business Requirements from Unstructured Texts Using Natural Language Processing

## Introduction

The extraction of business requirements from unstructured text remains a significant challenge in software engineering and requirements engineering. Stakeholders often express needs in natural language, producing documents that are dense, variable, and difficult to process automatically. The ambiguity inherent in natural language, combined with the high cost of misclassifying requirement statements, motivates the need for systems that can infer not only whether text contains a requirement but also how confident and reliable that inference is. This work examines how modern natural language processing techniques can be combined with structured analysis and fuzzy inference to transform raw requirement text into interpretable representations that support automation in requirements engineering.

## Purpose

The primary objective is to design and evaluate an intelligent pipeline capable of detecting requirement statements within free-form text and of converting them into structured representations suitable for downstream analysis. The proposed system architecture focuses on separating requirement detection from entity extraction and relation analysis. In addition, the research aims to assess the potential of fuzzy logic to infer requirement confidence, priority, and quality from partially extracted information. A secondary aim is to demonstrate how fuzzy semantics can capture the uncertainty and gradations present in requirement statements, thereby improving the system's ability to handle ambiguous or incomplete inputs.

## Methodology

The study is grounded in a business requirement dataset containing 16,263 synthetically generated sentences annotated with requirement labels. The dataset is roughly balanced, with 8,746 sentences labeled as requirements and 7,517 sentences labeled as non-requirements. These data reflect a simulated but realistic mix of requirement-like and ordinary explanatory text, which is typical for business documents and stakeholder communications.

Text preprocessing included sentence segmentation, tokenization, and embedding generation. Word embeddings were obtained from a pre-trained distributional model and aggregated at the sentence level by averaging lexical vectors to produce dense text representations. Exploratory analysis used principal component analysis to project these vectors into a two-dimensional space for visualization and to inspect the separability of requirement and non-requirement classes.

Classification of requirement-bearing sentences was explored through multiple approaches. A gradient boosting classifier was trained on averaged embedding vectors to distinguish requirement sentences from non-requirement sentences. Feature selection was then applied with a random forest regressor to identify the most influential embedding dimensions, and a support vector machine was trained on this reduced feature set. In parallel, a traditional bag-of-words pipeline using TF-IDF with logistic regression was implemented as a baseline. The work also developed a transformer-based sequence classification approach using a pre-trained encoder, first for binary requirement detection and then for multiclass requirement typing through a hybrid strategy combining zero-shot pseudo-labeling with a four-class transformer model. This experimentation provided a comparative view of lexical, distributional, and contextual classification strategies.

Requirement structuring was addressed with a dedicated information extraction module. A transformer-backed parser was augmented with manually crafted entity patterns for stakeholder, system, action, object, condition, constraint, priority, and quality concepts. Syntactic dependency analysis provided additional structure by identifying main action verbs, subjects, direct objects, conditional clauses, and numeric constraints. The resulting extraction module populated a structured representation in which textual requirements were mapped into fields that approximate a requirements ontology.

A fuzzy inference layer was introduced to evaluate the extracted requirement representations. The layer defined measures of completeness and clarity based on the presence of core entities, constraint strength derived from temporal expressions, and stakeholder importance based on actor type. Confidence and priority were inferred through rule-based aggregation, while quality assessment penalized vagueness and rewarded completeness in combination with explicit constraints. Two implementations of the fuzzy layer were developed: one using custom heuristic functions and another using a dedicated fuzzy control system library to formalize the same linguistic rules.

The fuzzy logic design began by defining continuous variables for completeness, clarity, constraint strength, and stakeholder importance. Completeness was computed as the ratio of filled fields to the total number of target fields in the structured requirement representation. Clarity was modeled as a function of whether action, object, and stakeholder entities were present. Constraint strength was derived from numerical temporal expressions and interpreted as stronger when deadlines were tighter. Stakeholder importance mapped actor types such as administrator and manager to higher importance values while assigning moderate values to users and clients.

Membership functions were defined with triangular shapes for the linguistic categories low, medium, and high. For completeness and clarity, low values corresponded to sparse or missing core entities, medium values reflected partially specified requirements, and high values indicated that essential elements were present. For constraint strength and stakeholder importance, the membership functions encoded the intuition that stricter constraints and more critical stakeholders should push the inferred outcomes toward higher priority and confidence.

The fuzzy rules were expressed in natural language and formalized in the system. A confidence rule asserted that if completeness is high and clarity is high, then confidence should be high. Another rule assigned medium confidence when completeness is medium, and a third rule assigned low confidence when clarity is low. Priority rules associated high priority with either high constraint strength or high stakeholder importance, while medium priority was inferred when both constraint strength and stakeholder importance were moderate. Quality rules reduced the quality score when vague words such as fast or easy were present and increased quality when the requirement representation was complete and contained explicit constraints.

To implement these rules, the system used a fuzzy control library to create antecedent and consequent variables, define membership functions, and combine rules through fuzzy operators. The control system simulated input values and produced defuzzified outputs for confidence and priority. The resulting continuous outputs were discretized into semantic labels where appropriate and stored in the structured requirement representation alongside the extracted fields.

## Findings

The experiments demonstrated that requirement detection can be performed with reasonable accuracy using both embedding-based and TF-IDF approaches. The gradient boosting classifier on averaged embeddings was able to separate requirement statements from non-requirement text, and the subsequent support vector machine with feature selection showed that certain embedding dimensions contributed disproportionately to performance. The logistic regression TF-IDF baseline provided complementary evidence that lexical patterns capture requirement language effectively. The transformer-based classifier established a viable path toward multiclass requirement typing, suggesting that functional, non-functional, and constraint classes can be distinguished when sufficient labeled data is available.

The structured extraction stage revealed that named entity recognition alone is insufficient for robust requirement understanding. Combined use of explicit entity patterns and dependency parsing was necessary to infer the main action, subject, object, conditions, and constraints reliably. The constructed representations could be serialized into a structured form that includes stakeholder, system, action, object, conditions, constraints, priority, quality, and confidence. This serialization demonstrated that the extraction pipeline can bridge the gap between raw text and a format that is amenable to automated checking and downstream requirements analysis.

The fuzzy inference layer produced interpretable metadata for confidence, priority, and quality. Confidence values reflected the degree to which extracted requirements were complete and clear, while priority scores responded to the presence of strong constraints and critical stakeholders. Quality assessment penalized vague expressions and rewarded well-specified requirements that included explicit constraints. The parallel implementation using a fuzzy control system confirmed that the chosen rules were stable under both heuristic and formal fuzzy aggregation, demonstrating the robustness of the approach to uncertainty.

The detailed fuzzy logic formulation showed that uncertainty in requirement interpretation can be handled by soft membership values rather than by hard thresholds. This allowed the system to assign intermediate confidence levels to partially specified requirements and to distinguish between requirements that were clearly stated and those that were ambiguous. As a result, the system can support downstream decision-making by providing graded assessments instead of binary judgments.

## Conclusions

This research validates an integrated approach to requirements intelligence that combines natural language classification, entity and relation extraction, and fuzzy rule-based inference. The proposed architecture is capable of identifying requirement statements, extracting structured requirement elements, and assigning semantic metadata such as confidence, priority, and quality. The study shows that hybrid solutions which blend statistical NLP with rule-based linguistic analysis and fuzzy semantics are promising for the automation of business requirement processing.

The application of fuzzy logic is particularly valuable in this domain because it explicitly models uncertainty and vagueness, which are inherent in natural language requirements. By representing requirement confidence and priority as fuzzy variables and by applying linguistic rules, the system captures subtle gradations that are difficult to encode with purely crisp classifiers.

Future work should focus on acquiring richer annotated corpora, refining relation extraction methods for more complex requirement structures, and incorporating interactive human-in-the-loop validation to ensure that inferred metadata aligns with stakeholder intent. Further investigation is also needed into the calibration of fuzzy membership functions and rule weights so that the semantic metadata can be tuned for different domains and organizational contexts. Advancing these areas would further improve the practical applicability of requirement extraction systems in real-world software engineering contexts.
